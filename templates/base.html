Choose the Right Trekking Shoes

Invest in a pair of high-quality trekking shoes that provide good ankle support and are suitable for the terrain you'll be covering. Consider factors such as the type of trails (rocky, muddy, etc.) and the duration of your trek. Make sure your shoes are comfortable and well-fitted to prevent blisters and discomfort during long hikes. Break them in before your trek to avoid any surprises on the trail.

Use Trekking Poles (Sticks) for Stability

Trekking poles provide stability and reduce the strain on your knees and lower body, especially during descents. They can also help with balance on uneven terrain. Adjust the length of the poles according to the slope of the trail. Shorten them for uphill climbs and lengthen them for downhill descents. This helps in maintaining a natural arm position.

Carry and Use a Map and Compass (and/or GPS)

Always carry a detailed map of the trekking area along with a compass. Familiarize yourself with the route before starting the trek, and know how to use the map and compass to navigate. If possible, use a GPS device or a trekking app on your phone to track your progress. However, do not solely rely on electronic devices, as they can run out of battery or lose signal in remote areas.
